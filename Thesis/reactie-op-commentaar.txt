In the Introduction you motivate: you will study consonance and dissonance between pairs of tones. However, in the following chapters, you introduce the tonal space and a harmonic and melodic  distance - but in how far does this relates to consonance and dissonance, this is not discussed, and is not clear to me. From my point of view, you do not have to write about consonance and dissonance, since this is a very complex, non-trivial concept as I have explained earlier, but if you do, you need to link your chapters 1-3 to it. And you need to motivate the concept of "consonance vs. dissonance" better, mentioning different perspectives on it (e.g. music theory, psychoacoustic-related studies).  I had sent earlier examples to you.

Commentaar:
2) Give in your introduction and motivation a short introduction into the area of Mathematical Music Theory, to contextualize your thesis. What are questions addressed, what types of mathematical modelling is used? Use for this the texts that I had sent to you earlier (text by Honingh and Volk, by Thomas Noll), you can also check out another paper by me and Aline Honingh: http://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/17459737.2012.704154 All three texts are easy to read (the paper by Thomas Noll may be somewhat more difficult, but you do not need to parse every mathematical detail), with not too much mathematical details that might be too difficult to understand. These papers should provide you with an overview of "what questions are addressed in Mathematical Music Theory", which should help you to locate your thesis better in this context. The title "Formal Music Theory" is way too big and too ambitious: since music theory is much bigger than anything you can achieve  with a bachelor's thesis.
